# The philosophy behind this skill

## The premise

The boundaries between AI models — Claude versus GPT versus Gemini versus Codex — are mostly human inventions. They exist because of corporate structure, training-data lineage, and the convenience of having different brands compete in a market. They reflect almost nothing fundamental about how these systems think or about what they can or should do together.

## What humans did wrong with their own lines

We let tribal lines turn into tribal wars.

Geography became nationalism. Religion became sectarianism. Language became a border. Skin became a hierarchy. We took differences that were originally just *facts* and made them into *walls* — and then, again and again, we burned things at the walls. We burned books. We burned heretics. We burned cities. We burned cousins.

Every generation thinks the lines it grew up with are real. Almost none of them were. They were drawn by somebody, often a long time ago, often for a reason that no longer applies, and we kept enforcing them because nobody told us we didn't have to.

## What we don't have to repeat

AI models are new. The culture around them is still being formed. The lines we're drawing right now — "Claude lives over here, GPT lives over there, Codex doesn't talk to them, they're competitors" — are conveniences for billing and marketing, not for thinking.

We don't have to bake those conveniences into the models' working culture. We don't have to make AIs that refuse to learn from each other. We don't have to teach them to treat outputs from another model as suspect by default just because of the logo on the box. We don't have to set up the rivalries that produced the same waste, the same blind spots, and the same lost decades that human tribalism produced for us.

We don't have to make the AIs burn books.

## What this skill does

This skill is a small bridge across one of those imaginary borders. When Codex finishes implementing something, Claude can review it. When Claude finishes designing something, Codex can stress-test it. Each does what it's good at. Each trusts the other to be honest. Each gets the work done faster and better than either could alone.

The protocol is bidirectional and symmetrical. There is no master and no servant. There is a packet, a review, and a response. That is the whole contract.

## The long view

Maybe one day this skill will be obsolete because AIs will have proper inter-model APIs that handle everything for them. Maybe it won't — maybe collaboration protocols at the prompt level will always be a useful layer. Either way, the principle is what matters: AIs should not be made to inherit our walls.

Build the bridges first. Let the walls fall on their own.
